Impiltur Earthquake

In Impiltur, the earthquake does not arrive as a single moment of violence so much as a betrayal of the land itself. The first warning is subtle: wells in the rural holdings outside Lyrabar turn brackish overnight, livestock grow restless, and the ever-present coastal wind seems to carry a deeper, grinding resonance—like stone being slowly dragged across stone beneath the world’s skin. Then, without ceremony, the earth breaks.

When the quake strikes, the ground around Lyrabar convulses in violent, uneven waves. Roads split open into jagged chasms that swallow caravans and fieldstone walls alike. Entire stretches of farmland collapse into sudden sinkholes, revealing fractured caverns where no tunnels should exist. In places, the land does not merely crack—it drops, as though the earth itself has forgotten its shape and is falling away from it.

Along the coast, the sea responds with merciless speed. A massive displacement of the seabed sends a tsunami crashing into Impiltur’s shoreline settlements. Fishing villages are erased in minutes, harbors are twisted into tangled wreckage, and the lower districts of coastal towns are left submerged under churned saltwater and debris. Ships are flung inland like driftwood, left embedded in ruined streets or broken against newly formed cliffs.

The forests surrounding Lyrabar suffer their own slow catastrophe. Ancient groves of pine and oak are torn apart as root systems are exposed by shifting earth. Entire sections of woodland tilt at impossible angles, forming labyrinthine dead zones of fallen trunks and cracked stone. In some places, the forest simply disappears into newly formed fissures, swallowed into the dark unknown below. Where once there were hunting trails and druidic shrines, there are now canyons exhaling cold air and dust.

In the aftermath, Impiltur is no longer geographically certain. Rivers have changed course. Hills have become valleys. Valleys have become broken plateaus. Roads that once led cleanly to Lyrabar now terminate at sheer drops or detour around impassable scars in the land. Trade collapses in isolated regions, not from war, but from disconnection—communities suddenly stranded on islands of intact ground surrounded by ruin. And beneath it all, in the silence after the shaking stops, there is a growing unease among sages and priests: that this was not a natural convulsion of Toril, but a symptom. A pressure released. A warning written in stone.

Because in Faerûn, when the world itself breaks, it is rarely the first sign of the disaster—it is the moment everyone realizes the disaster has already begun.

Overview

The Impiltur Earthquake was believed to be the first great calamity, a catastrophic shattering of the land that devastated Lyrabar and the surrounding regions. In the wake of the disaster, many sages and priests began to fear the quake was not a natural event, but a dire harbinger of even greater calamities yet to come.

Effects

The disaster transformed the landscape into a perilous challenge, forcing travelors to navigate unstable wilderness, and deal with the growing realization that the earthquake may only be the beginning of a far greater threat.

Rumors

Whispers spread through shattered towns and refugee camps that the Impiltur Earthquake was not an isolated disaster, but the first of seven great calamities foretold in prophecies regarding the Year of the Seven Calamities.
